EGR 101: Engineering Design & Communication

Innovation-Level Expectations

Duke Pratt's first-year design course sets exceptionally high standards, with some student projects achieving patent-pending status. This demonstrates the course's commitment to fostering genuine innovation and preparing students for professional-level engineering challenges from their very first semester.

Collaborative Engineering Practice

The course emphasizes teamwork, leadership, and communication skills essential for professional engineering practice. Students work in collaborative teams, learning to coordinate complex projects, manage timelines, and communicate technical concepts effectively to diverse audiences including peers, instructors, and industry professionals.

"Embrace Challenges" Philosophy

Central to the course philosophy is teaching students to "embrace challenges" and work within real-world constraints. The curriculum builds confidence through systematic methodology, teaching that complex engineering problems are solvable through collaborative effort, iterative design, and learning from failure as an essential part of the process.

Professional Engineering Standards

The course mirrors professional engineering practice with systematic design methodology, formal documentation requirements, constraint analysis, and iterative prototyping. Students develop the "I Can Do This" mindset that builds self-efficacy and prepares them for advanced engineering coursework and professional careers.

Design Challenge

Problem Identification

Problem Definition Within EGR 101 Framework

Following Duke Pratt's systematic design methodology, our team conducted comprehensive user research and problem analysis to identify inefficiencies in manual bucket cleaning processes. This aligned with the course's emphasis on real-world problem identification and the professional engineering practice of understanding user needs before proposing solutions.

Collaborative Requirements Development

Working as a collaborative team, we established quantifiable design criteria including cleaning coverage targets (95%+ surface area), cycle time goals (under 3 minutes), and water efficiency parameters. This process exemplified the course's emphasis on teamwork and systematic constraint analysis within real-world limitations.

Innovation-Level Solution Architecture

Developed comprehensive system architecture combining mechanical rotation, pressurized water delivery, and automated positioning—demonstrating the innovation-level thinking that characterizes Duke's first-year design course. Created detailed specifications and integration plans that would meet the course's professional engineering standards.

Engineering Solution

Automated Washing System

Mechanical System Design & Professional Practice

Following EGR 101's systematic design methodology, I led the mechanical system development including DC motor selection with gear reduction for optimal cleaning performance. Applied professional engineering standards in fabricating mounting brackets and positioning systems, demonstrating the course's emphasis on real-world engineering practice and attention to detail.

Fluid Systems Integration & Innovation

Designed and implemented a comprehensive water delivery system using engineering principles taught in EGR 101. The pressurized system with filtration and recycling capabilities exemplified the innovation-level thinking the course fosters, going beyond basic requirements to create an environmentally conscious and efficient solution.

Control System Development & Testing

Programmed Arduino-based automation system with safety interlocks and user interfaces, demonstrating the interdisciplinary approach emphasized in EGR 101. Conducted iterative testing following the course's "learning from failure" philosophy to optimize performance and ensure reliable operation across multiple cleaning cycles.
