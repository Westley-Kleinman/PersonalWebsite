Elevon - 3D Topographic Maps

From High School Project to Commercial Business

Founded and operate Elevon, transforming real elevation data into precision 3D printed topographic maps. Started as a high school project, now serving race directors with custom trophy maps and trail enthusiasts with detailed terrain models.

Problem Identification

The Challenge

Trail Visualization Gap

Race directors and trail enthusiasts struggled with the limitations of 2D maps for understanding terrain complexity. Traditional maps couldn't effectively communicate elevation changes, trail difficulty variations, or the true physical challenge of routes. Event organizers needed meaningful, tangible representations of their race courses.

Existing Solution Limitations

Commercial 3D terrain models were expensive, generic, and didn't include trail-specific information. Available options couldn't incorporate trail difficulty data, custom route highlighting, or be produced at accessible price points for small race events. The technology existed but wasn't accessible for the trail running community.

Technical Requirements

The solution needed to combine real topographic elevation data with trail route information, incorporate difficulty classifications, maintain geographic accuracy, and be producible using consumer-grade 3D printing technology while remaining cost-effective for small orders.

Technical Implementation

How I Built Elevon

Step 1: Trail Data Collection & Processing

I start with trail maps provided by race directors or screenshot from Trailforks. Using a custom web application I developed (elevon.onrender.com), I create black and white images separating trail difficulties - each difficulty level becomes a distinct black trail on white background image. This automated process ensures consistent data formatting for downstream processing.

Step 2: Vector Conversion & Preparation

Each difficulty-specific bitmap image is converted to SVG format using Inkscape, creating precise vector paths that can be scaled and manipulated without quality loss. This vectorization step is crucial for accurate 3D extrusion and ensures clean trail edges in the final printed model.

Step 3: Topographic Data Integration

Real elevation data is sourced from TouchTerrain, providing accurate geographic information for the specific region. This data forms the foundation 3D terrain mesh that accurately represents the actual landscape topography with proper elevation scaling and geographic precision.

Step 4: 3D Trail Extrusion

In Blender, I import each SVG trail path and extrude them to create 3D trail representations. Each difficulty level gets its own extrusion height and will eventually become a different color in the final print. The trails are precisely positioned to align with the corresponding locations on the topographic base mesh.

Step 5: Terrain Integration & Boolean Operations

The topographic mesh undergoes Z-axis scaling to exaggerate landscape features for better visual impact and printability. I perform boolean intersection operations between the extruded trail paths and the scaled terrain mesh, ensuring trails follow the actual ground contours while maintaining their distinct elevation characteristics.

Step 6: Alignment & Final Assembly

All mesh components are carefully aligned to ensure proper registration between terrain base and trail overlays. Each element is verified for printability, including overhang angles, minimum feature sizes, and structural integrity. The final assembled model maintains geographic accuracy while optimizing for 3D printing constraints.

Step 7: Print Preparation

The final aligned models are imported into BambuStudio for print preparation. I configure the slicer for 4-color printing on my BambuLab printer, assigning different colors to the terrain base and each trail difficulty level. Print settings are optimized for detail resolution while maintaining reasonable print times and material usage.

Step 8: Multi-Color Production

The BambuLab printer handles automatic filament changes to produce maps with distinct colors for different trail difficulties and terrain base. This eliminates post-processing painting steps while ensuring consistent color separation and professional appearance. Each map typically uses 4 colors: base terrain plus three difficulty levels.

Step 9: Quality Assurance & Delivery

Each printed map undergoes quality inspection for dimensional accuracy, color consistency, and structural integrity. Maps are carefully packaged to prevent damage during shipping and include documentation explaining the trail difficulty color coding and any specific route features highlighted in the model.
